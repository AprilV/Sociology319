Case Study: Training an AI Partner

What It Actually Takes to Collaborate with AI: The Hidden Work Behind the H.C. Lombardo App

📝 Based on: My own experience building the app

The Myth vs. Reality

The Myth: "Just ask AI and it does the work. Anyone can use it."

The Reality: Over 6 weeks building the H.C. Lombardo App, I spent as much time teaching the AI how to work with me as I did building features. This case study documents what that training actually looked like.

Important context: While this project took 6 weeks, I didn't start from zero. I've been using AI for various tasks over the past couple of years, ChatGPT since its launch in 2022. I learned through trial and error, making mistakes, figuring out what works. This 6-week partnership built on years of prior AI experience. Someone starting completely fresh would face an even steeper learning curve.

🛠️ What "Training" Actually Meant

1. Created Documentation Files for AI to Review

What I did: Created 15+ markdown (.md) files documenting project conventions, workflows, and best practices. Why? Because AI doesn't automatically remember things across sessions. The files gave it something to review.

SOCIOLOGY.md - Research observations
QUICK_START_HCL.md - Setup instructions
PRODUCTION_DEPLOYMENT.md - Deployment workflow
DATABASE_SCHEMA.md - Schema documentation
... and 11+ more files

2. Repeated Explicit Instructions Until Patterns Stuck

Early project (Weeks 1-2): Every single time I made a change, I had to say:

"Make sure you backup the database first, then commit the changes to Git, then push to the remote repository."

After weeks of repetition: AI started suggesting backups proactively. But it didn't happen automatically. It happened because I trained it through consistent corrections.

3. Used VS Code Environment (Not Web Chat)

Critical detail: This collaboration happened in VS Code with GitHub Copilot, which gives AI access to project files, not in a web chat like ChatGPT. The AI could:

  • Read my .md documentation files
  • See the entire project structure
  • Reference previous code changes
  • Maintain context across sessions

This is model-specific. I used Claude Sonnet 4.5 in VS Code. Other AI models, other environments, I have no idea if they'd behave the same way. This isn't universal AI behavior.

4. Invested Time When It Made Mistakes

Reality check: AI forgot context. It made mistakes. I lost 5 hours of work once when it misunderstood the project state. But instead of quitting, I:

  • Corrected the errors explicitly
  • Updated documentation so it wouldn't happen again
  • Adjusted my workflow to compensate
  • Kept trying

📅 How Communication Changed Over 6 Weeks

Week 1-2: Instructor Mode

Me: "Make sure you backup the database first, then commit the changes to Git, then push to the remote repository."

Step-by-step instructions. High oversight. Constant verification.

Week 3-4: Fewer Reminders

Me: "Let's add the portfolio link and make sure to backup first."

AI started anticipating some steps. Still needed occasional reminders.

Week 5-6: Shorthand

Me: "prod, backup, push"

Three words. AI understood the full workflow. This took weeks of training to achieve.

💡 Why This Explains the Digital Divide

1. Many People Try Once Without Success

When I talk to people about AI, they often say: "I tried ChatGPT once and it wasn't helpful." They expected it to work perfectly immediately. Week 1 was frustrating for me too. But they didn't stick around to see what happens after weeks of training investment.

2. There's No "How to Train AI" Guide

I figured out the .md file strategy through trial and error. I learned to give explicit instructions through frustration when AI forgot things. Nobody taught me this. Most people don't have the technical background or patience to figure it out themselves.

3. Environment Matters (And Most People Use Web Chat)

My setup (VS Code + GitHub Copilot + Claude Sonnet 4.5) allowed AI to access project files and maintain context. Web-based ChatGPT doesn't work this way. Most people are using the free web version with no file access, no project context, and limited memory. Their experience is fundamentally different from mine.

4. The Payoff Takes Time

By Week 6, I could say "prod, backup, push" and AI executed a complex workflow. But that efficiency cost me weeks of training investment. Without that time investment and guidance, reaching proficiency is difficult.

🔗 What This Means for Bridging the Divide

This case study suggests that AI literacy requires more than just access. It involves:

  • Training skills: Documentation, explicit instruction, pattern reinforcement
  • Technical environment: Tools like VS Code offer advantages over basic web chat
  • Time investment: Weeks of practice before seeing efficiency gains
  • Patience for mistakes: Tolerance for setbacks (I lost 5 hours once but kept going)

What I learned applies most directly to professionals trying to use AI for complex, sustained work, not casual users checking facts or getting quick answers. The training investment I made (documentation, explicit instruction patterns, weeks of practice) may be what's required for professional-level AI collaboration. As more workplaces require employees to use AI as a tool or collaborator, understanding what that training actually involves becomes critical. The divide exists around who has access to this knowledge and support.

⚠️ Limitations of This Case Study

  • Sample size: N=1 (my experience only)
  • Model-specific: Claude Sonnet 4.5 in VS Code (other AI models may behave differently)
  • My background: 20+ years IT experience, technical training skills, PLUS ~3 years of prior AI use (since ChatGPT launched in 2022). Not representative of average users
  • No formal data collection: Observational notes, not controlled experiment

This is not proof of universal AI behavior. It's documentation of what I had to do to make this specific AI work effectively in this environment, after years of learning AI through mistakes and experimentation. Someone with no prior AI experience would face significantly more challenges.
