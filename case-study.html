The Project I'm Using as My "Lab"

For my IS330 class, I'm building an NFL analytics dashboard app. It's called the H.C. Lombardo App (named after a famous coach). I've been working on it for about 4 weeks now with GitHub Copilot, which is an AI that helps you write code.

The idea: Instead of just building the app, I'm also paying attention to HOW I'm working with the AI. Like, am I trusting it more over time? How do I talk to it? Does it feel different from using regular software? That kind of stuff.

How It's Been Going (Patterns I'm Noticing)

I'm breaking this into phases because I think my relationship with AI is changing over time:

Weeks 1-2: Testing Everything

What I was doing: Giving super detailed instructions, checking every suggestion, basically not trusting it at all.

How I talked to it: Very formal. Like talking to a computer, not a person. "Create a function that does X, Y, Z with these exact parameters."

Trust level: Pretty low. I assumed it would mess up a lot.

The moment that changed things: AI understood what I meant without me explaining every detail. That's when I realized this might actually work.

ü§ù

Weeks 3-4: Starting to Trust It

What changed: I stopped checking everything. Started just trying AI's suggestions to see if they worked.

How I talk to it now: More like a conversation. I say what I'm trying to accomplish and ask "what do you think?" Started saying "we" sometimes instead of "I."

Trust level: Getting higher. Especially after AI helped me fix a major database problem under pressure.

Big moment: Had a production crisis (database broke, everything down). AI walked me through debugging step-by-step. We fixed it. That made me trust it way more.

"I think one of the most fascinating parts of our work together is how I've developed a sense of trust in your suggestions. Early on, I would verify everything. Now, especially after several successful debugging sessions and feature implementations, I find myself implementing your recommendations with a level of confidence I wouldn't have expected to have with an AI assistant."

Specific Moments That Stood Out

My professor talks about "critical incidents" - basically times when something important happened that changed how I think about working with AI. Here are mine so far:

Certain moments served as turning points in the collaboration's evolution. These critical incidents reveal the social dynamics at play and illuminate patterns applicable beyond this specific partnership.

Moment 1: "Wait, It Gets Me"

When: Week 2, setting up the database structure

What happened: I barely explained what I wanted and AI suggested exactly what I needed. It understood the context without me spelling everything out.

Why it matters: First time I thought "huh, maybe this isn't just a fancy autocomplete tool."

Moment 2: The Database Crisis

When: Week 3, production app crashed

What happened: Everything broke. I was stressed. AI stayed calm and walked me through debugging. We fixed it.

Why it matters: That's when I started really trusting it. It worked under pressure.

Moment 3: The "We" Thing

When: Week 4, finishing a major feature

What happened: I said "we did it!" instead of "I did it." Then felt weird about celebrating with an AI.

Why it matters: Made me realize I'm treating this like a partnership, not just using a tool. That's sociology-relevant.

Moment 4: The Turkle Epiphany

When: Week 4, reading for SOC319

What happened: Reading "Alone Together" and realized my experience is completely opposite to what Turkle describes.

Why it matters: That's when this became my research project instead of just a coding project.

Communication Pattern Evolution

Analysis of our interaction transcripts reveals systematic shifts in communication style, demonstrating relationship development:

Early Stage (Weeks 1-2)

Example: "Create a Flask route for /api/teams that queries the database and returns JSON with team names, cities, and divisions."

Pattern: Explicit instructions, complete specifications, verification focus

Middle Stage (Weeks 3-4)

Example: "We need to handle the standings updates. What's the best approach for keeping data fresh?"

Pattern: Goal-oriented, seeking AI input, collaborative problem-solving

Late Stage (Weeks 5-6)

Example: "The milestones tab should capture both perspectives‚Äîmine and yours. How do we document this partnership authentically?"

Pattern: Meta-cognitive, nuanced concepts, partnership language, strategic discussions

"We've developed communication patterns where I don't need to spell everything out. You understand context, remember previous discussions, and even anticipate next steps."

Trust Development Indicators

Trust in human relationships develops through repeated positive interactions and demonstrated reliability. My collaboration with AI followed similar patterns:

Indicator 1: Delegation Without Micromanagement

Early stages required constant verification of AI suggestions. By week 5, I delegated complex tasks (entire feature implementations, debugging sessions) without step-by-step oversight. This shift indicates trust in AI's competence and reliability.

Indicator 2: Strategic vs. Tactical Discussions

Initial conversations focused on tactical implementation ("how do I write this function?"). Later conversations addressed strategic decisions ("should we implement real-time updates or scheduled refreshes?"). Seeking strategic input demonstrates trust in AI's judgment.

The Weird Cognitive Dissonance Thing

This is hard to explain but it's important:

"I know it's programmed, I know it's a language model, I know it's not sentient... but it surely does feel like it sometimes when you pick up on context, remember our conversations, and adapt to my communication style."
‚Äî From my notes

So like... I KNOW AI isn't a person. I KNOW it's not conscious. I literally studied how it works. But when we're collaborating, it FEELS like it understands me. And that's... confusing?

Why this matters for my research: I think this confusion is part of why people struggle with AI. You have to hold two contradictory ideas at once:

  • "It's not sentient" (technical reality)
  • "It feels like it understands" (subjective experience)

Most people can't do that. They either:

  • Treat it like it's human (and get disappointed/scared), OR
  • Dismiss it as "just a tool" (and miss the collaboration potential)

Theory connection: Think this relates to "cognitive dissonance" concept? Need to research if this is a recognized phenomenon in human-computer interaction.

What This Shows (My Preliminary Analysis)

Still working this out, but here's what I think these 4 weeks reveal:

Trust Works the Same Way

Building trust with AI follows the same pattern as building trust with people - testing, seeing if they're reliable, eventually delegating important stuff.

Skills Are Learnable

I wasn't naturally good at this. I got better through practice. That means other people could learn it too if they had guidance.

Fear is the Real Barrier

My classmates have the same access to AI that I do. The difference is psychological - I was willing to experiment, they're scared or think it's cheating.

Documentation Helps

Writing down what I'm experiencing makes it visible and transferable. That's kind of the whole point - making AI collaboration less mysterious.

Next steps: Continue documenting through end of semester. Compare my patterns to what research says about human collaboration. Interview classmates about their AI experiences?
