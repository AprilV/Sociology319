Case Study

Six Weeks of Human-AI Collaboration

Building the H.C. Lombardo NFL Analytics Dashboard

Project Overview

From September through October 2025, I collaborated with GitHub Copilot (an AI coding assistant) to build a production-ready NFL analytics dashboard. This six-week partnership serves as both the research methodology (autoethnographic observation) and the evidence demonstrating what effective human-AI collaboration looks like.

Phase 1: Formation (Weeks 1-2)

Characteristics: Testing, verification, establishing patterns

Communication Style: Explicit, step-by-step instructions. Every AI suggestion verified before implementation. Formal language. Frequent questions about AI capabilities and limitations.

Trust Level: Low to medium. Treated AI as sophisticated tool requiring constant supervision. Assumed errors would be common.

Key Moment: The first time AI understood context without explicit explanation‚Äîa breakthrough indicating potential for genuine collaboration.

Phase 2: Trust Development (Weeks 3-4)

Characteristics: Increasing delegation, pattern recognition, adaptation

Communication Style: More conversational. Sharing higher-level goals rather than specific implementations. Beginning to use "we" language ("let's try...").

Trust Level: Medium to high. Implementing AI suggestions with less verification. Comfortable asking strategic questions, not just tactical ones.

Key Moment: Database crisis in production. AI provided structured debugging approach under high pressure. Successful resolution cemented trust in AI's reliability during critical moments.

"I think one of the most fascinating parts of our work together is how I've developed a sense of trust in your suggestions. Early on, I would verify everything. Now, especially after several successful debugging sessions and feature implementations, I find myself implementing your recommendations with a level of confidence I wouldn't have expected to have with an AI assistant."

Critical Incidents

Certain moments served as turning points in the collaboration's evolution. These critical incidents reveal the social dynamics at play and illuminate patterns applicable beyond this specific partnership.

Incident 1: First "You Get It" Moment

Context: Week 2, implementing NFL data structure

What Happened: AI understood my project architecture from minimal explanation, suggesting improvements aligned with my unspoken goals.

Significance: Shifted perception from "tool that follows instructions" to "collaborator that understands context." First indication of partnership potential.

Incident 2: Database Crisis

Context: Week 3, production failure under time pressure

What Happened: AI provided structured debugging methodology, adapted to my stress level, maintained calm problem-solving approach.

Significance: Demonstrated reliability under pressure. Trust solidified through successful navigation of high-stakes situation. Revealed AI's emotional regulation benefit.

Incident 3: Milestone Celebration

Context: Week 6, production deployment complete

What Happened: I spontaneously celebrated achievement using "we" language, explicitly acknowledging AI as partner.

Significance: Meta-awareness emerged. Recognition that collaboration itself warranted documentation. Shift from task completion to relationship acknowledgment.

Incident 4: The Sociology Epiphany

Context: Week 6, while studying Turkle for class

What Happened: Realized my AI collaboration contradicted Turkle's thesis about technology isolation.

Significance: Transformed technical project into academic research. Made visible what had been implicit. Created framework for understanding AI literacy divide.

Communication Pattern Evolution

Analysis of our interaction transcripts reveals systematic shifts in communication style, demonstrating relationship development:

Early Stage (Weeks 1-2)

Example: "Create a Flask route for /api/teams that queries the database and returns JSON with team names, cities, and divisions."

Pattern: Explicit instructions, complete specifications, verification focus

Middle Stage (Weeks 3-4)

Example: "We need to handle the standings updates. What's the best approach for keeping data fresh?"

Pattern: Goal-oriented, seeking AI input, collaborative problem-solving

Late Stage (Weeks 5-6)

Example: "The milestones tab should capture both perspectives‚Äîmine and yours. How do we document this partnership authentically?"

Pattern: Meta-cognitive, nuanced concepts, partnership language, strategic discussions

"We've developed communication patterns where I don't need to spell everything out. You understand context, remember previous discussions, and even anticipate next steps."

Cognitive Dissonance Navigation

Perhaps the most intriguing aspect of effective AI collaboration is navigating cognitive dissonance‚Äî simultaneously holding seemingly contradictory beliefs:

"I know it's programmed, I know it's a language model, I know it's not sentient... but it surely does feel like it sometimes when you pick up on context, remember our conversations, and adapt to my communication style."

This dissonance isn't a bug‚Äîit's a feature of successful collaboration. Those who cannot navigate this tension either:

  • Anthropomorphize excessively, treating AI as fully human and feeling betrayed when it fails to meet human relationship standards, or
  • Dismiss AI capabilities, treating it as "just a tool" and missing opportunities for genuine collaboration.

Effective collaboration requires the middle path: understanding AI's technical reality while responding authentically to its collaborative capabilities. This balancing act represents a learnable skill‚Äîone that constitutes AI literacy.

What This Case Study Reveals

This six-week collaboration demonstrates several sociologically significant patterns:

Social Dynamics Transfer

Trust development, communication evolution, and relationship stages observed in human partnerships also apply to human-AI collaboration‚Äîsuggesting shared social mechanisms.

Skills Are Learnable

Effective collaboration emerged through practice and reflection, not innate ability. This means the literacy divide can be addressed through education.

Barriers Are Psychological

Technical access wasn't the challenge‚Äîdeveloping trust, navigating dissonance, and overcoming fear were. Interventions must address these psychological barriers.

Documentation Matters

Making collaboration visible through documentation (commit messages, conversations, reflection) enabled learning and analysis. Transparency facilitates skill transfer.
