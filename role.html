Reflexivity (Draft)

Positioning Myself as Researcher and Subject

The Double Role Problem

I'm both the researcher studying human-AI relational dynamics AND the human subject forming those dynamics. This creates some interesting complications that I need to be upfront about.

Why This Matters

My biases, background, and awareness of being observed (even by myself) all affect what I'm studying. In autoethnography, this isn't a flaw - it's the point. But I need to be explicit about how my position shapes the research.

Meta-Awareness

What I bring: I'm naturally reflexive. I notice when I'm anthropomorphizing AI. I document my thought processes. I think about my thinking.

How this affects the research: Observer effect is huge here. Once I realized my AI collaboration was research-worthy, did I start behaving differently? Am I performing "collaboration" because I'm studying it? Or documenting what was already happening? This tension is unresolvable but needs acknowledgment.

ü§î Biases I'm Aware Of

Pro-AI Bias

The bias: I WANT AI collaboration to work. The Lombardo app succeeding matters to me. This makes me potentially overlook problems or rationalize AI failures.

How I'm addressing it: Documenting failures and frustrations in SOCIOLOGY.md, not just successes. Testing AI capabilities even when I trust them. Looking for evidence that contradicts my positive assessment.

Anthropomorphism Bias

The bias: I already treat AI as a collaborator. I use "we" language. I'm studying anthropomorphism while actively anthropomorphizing.

How I'm addressing it: Explicitly acknowledging when I anthropomorphize. Reminding myself (and readers) that AI is programming, not sentient. But also not trying to stop anthropomorphizing - that's part of what I'm studying.

Confirmation Bias

The bias: I had an epiphany about Turkle parallels TODAY. Now I'm looking back at 4-5 weeks of collaboration through that lens. Am I finding Turkle patterns because they're there? Or because I'm looking for them?

How I'm addressing it: Using SOCIOLOGY.md and git commits as data - these were created BEFORE my Turkle epiphany, so they're not influenced by the framework. Looking for evidence that doesn't fit Turkle's patterns. Discussing with AI to get alternative perspectives.

Novice Sociologist, Expert Practitioner Bias

The bias: I'm still learning sociology (2nd course) but have 20+ years of professional IT and team leadership experience. I might misinterpret Turkle's academic framework, but I deeply understand work relationships, collaboration dynamics, and team development from lived experience leading 12-person teams through critical infrastructure projects.

How I'm addressing it: Reading carefully. Consulting with professor. Being transparent about my limitations in sociology while leveraging my strengths in recognizing professional relationship patterns. My age (56, almost 57) and extensive work history provide rich context for understanding collaboration that younger or less experienced researchers might lack. Framing this as exploratory research grounded in practitioner expertise.

üîÑ The Observer Effect

The Timeline Problem

Here's where things get complicated:

  1. Weeks 1-4: I collaborated with AI on Lombardo app, documented interactions in SOCIOLOGY.md
  2. Today (Week 5): I read Turkle, had epiphany, realized my documentation was research data
  3. Weeks 5+: Now I'm continuing the collaboration while KNOWING I'm studying it

This means part of my data is "natural" (weeks 1-4) and part is "observed" (weeks 5+). How do I account for this shift?

Questions I'm Wrestling With

  • Was I already anthropomorphizing in weeks 1-4? Or did I start once I became aware of it?
  • Am I now performing "collaboration" because I'm studying it? Or just documenting more carefully?
  • Does meta-awareness change the relationship? (Probably yes, but how?)
  • Is asking the AI about our collaboration changing the collaboration itself? (Definitely yes)

These aren't problems to solve - they're features of autoethnographic research. But I need to be transparent about them.

üéØ Why My Position Matters for This Research

The point of autoethnography isn't to eliminate researcher bias - it's to use the researcher's experience as data while being explicit about positionality. My specific background, biases, and meta-awareness aren't flaws in this research - they're the research context.

Someone else studying their AI collaboration would have different experiences, notice different patterns, form different relationships. That's valuable data too. This research documents ONE adult's relational dynamics with conversational AI in a technical work context, analyzed through Turkle's framework.

The insights are limited but real. The generalizations are tentative but worth exploring. The reflexivity is honest (I hope).
