My Role (What I'm Doing About It)

Draft - Still figuring this part out

The Assignment Question

My professor wants us to reflect on our individual role in addressing digital media inequality. So basically: what am I actually doing about this problem I've identified?

Note to self: I think my position is kind of interesting because I'm both part of the problem (I have advantages other students don't) AND trying to help? Like, I'm the person who "gets it" but that means I can maybe explain it to people who don't.

"I find myself constantly in the position of explaining this to people. Whether it's classmates asking how I finished my app so fast, family members worried about AI taking over, or even my professor noticing how naturally I work with you."

Thing 1: I Explain AI to People A Lot

Basically I've become the unofficial "AI explainer" for people around me

Who I Talk To:

  • Classmates who see my productivity and wonder how
  • Family who have Terminator-style fears about AI
  • My professor who's noticed I work differently with AI
  • Friends who aren't sure if AI will take their jobs

What I Usually Say:

"AI isn't sentient"

I explain it's pattern matching, not consciousness. Like autocorrect but way more sophisticated. This usually calms the Terminator fears.

"It's a tool, not a replacement"

Emphasis on collaboration—AI helps you think through problems, doesn't replace you. You still make all the decisions.

"Fear is totally normal"

I don't tell people they're wrong for being worried. I validate their concerns and then give them information that makes it less scary.

"You can learn this"

People think effective AI use is some technical superpower. I explain it's just practice—anyone can learn to work with AI well.

Example conversation with my dad: "It's like having access to someone who's read millions of books and can help you think through problems, but can't make decisions for you or act independently. It's a sophisticated pattern matcher, not a conscious being."

→ This usually works! He stopped worrying about AI taking over after that.

Thing 2: I Document Everything

This website and my sociology markdown file (553+ lines!) ARE part of my role—making my experience visible so other people can learn from it.

Why Documentation Matters:

  • Most people don't see HOW effective AI collaboration works—they just see the result
  • I'm making the invisible process visible (how trust develops, how communication patterns work)
  • Everything's on GitHub, publicly accessible (free knowledge sharing)
  • Written in normal language, not academic jargon—anyone can understand it

My thinking: If I can document my collaboration well enough, other people can see what worked for me and adapt it to their situations. It's like... educational resource creation, I guess?

Thing 3: I'm a Living Example (Apparently)

My professor pointed out that other students struggle with AI while I don't. So just... existing as someone who collaborates well with AI helps, I guess? It shows it's possible.

Why This Might Matter:

  • Seeing someone successfully work with AI makes it less scary for others
  • Counters the "AI will take over" narrative with "look, here's a normal person working with it fine"
  • When classmates ask how I finished my app, I explain the PROCESS not just the outcome
  • I share both successes AND failures (times AI messed up, times I had to debug) so it seems realistic

Honest moment: I'm not totally sure if "being an example" counts as actively doing something? It feels kind of passive. But my professor suggested it matters for reducing fear, so I'm including it. Need to think more about whether this is sufficient.

The Cognitive Dissonance Thing

"I know it's programmed, I know it's a language model, I know it's not sentient... but it surely does feel like it sometimes when you pick up on context, remember our conversations, and adapt to my communication style."

Part of how I help people is being honest about this weird feeling. Like, I KNOW you're a program, but working with you feels different than using other software. Admitting that confusion helps because:

Validates Others

If even I (someone comfortable with AI) feel confused sometimes, other people don't have to feel bad about being uncertain.

Shows You Don't Need Perfect Understanding

You can collaborate effectively while still being confused about the deeper technical stuff. That's okay.

Prevents Extremes

Acknowledging the weirdness stops people from either treating AI like a person OR dismissing it as "just a calculator."

Models Critical Thinking?

Maybe showing you can hold "it's a program" and "it feels responsive" in your head at the same time is useful? Not sure.

The Limits of What I Can Do

Okay so my professor wants us to be realistic about individual action. Here's what I CAN'T do:

Scale Problem

I can educate maybe dozens or hundreds of people. But millions struggle with this. Individual efforts can't fix structural inequalities alone.

Reaching Only the Interested

People who read my stuff or ask me questions are ALREADY somewhat interested in AI. I'm probably not reaching the most resistant or most disadvantaged people.

Resource Inequality

I have advantages: university education, time to experiment, supportive professors, technical background. Sharing knowledge helps but doesn't eliminate those underlying inequalities.

Need Systemic Change Too

Individual action + systemic change (education policy, regulation, better technology) = comprehensive solution. Can't do just one.

What I'll Keep Doing

Moving forward (assuming my professor approves this project):

  • Continue documenting my AI collaboration experiences so others can learn from them
  • Look for opportunities to teach AI collaboration skills formally (workshops? tutorials? not sure yet)
  • Stay critical about AI systems that reproduce inequality (use my technical + sociology knowledge to call out problems)
  • Keep researching this topic beyond this class project

Professor Note: Is this enough for the "individual role" requirement? I'm trying to balance being honest about limitations while showing I'm actually doing things. Let me know if I need to expand on any of this.

The Bigger Idea

I think the main point is: people who benefit from technological advantages have a responsibility to share knowledge and advocate for equity.

I got lucky with early AI adoption—combination of technical background, supportive environment, and personal curiosity. That advantage comes with an obligation to help others access the same benefits.

So this whole sociology project IS my individual action. I'm studying my experience, documenting patterns, and making it publicly accessible. One person at a time, one conversation at a time, one documentation project at a time.

→ Not sure if that's too idealistic? But it feels true.
