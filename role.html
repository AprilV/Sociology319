Reflexivity: My Role in the Divide

Positioning Myself as Bridge-Builder and Educator

The Central Tension

I'm both inside and outside the AI literacy divide:

  • Inside: At 56, I'm demographically part of the group AARP identifies as most skeptical of AI (AARP, 2025)
  • Outside: I'm in the 34% who use AI regularly, with 3 years of experience since ChatGPT launched in 2022
  • The Bridge: I understand both sides - the fear my classmates feel AND the productivity gains I've experienced

This unique position shapes everything about this research. I'm not an outside observer studying \"those people who don't use AI.\" I'm studying my own community from the perspective of someone who crossed the divide.

My Background: Why It Matters

1. Age and Demographics

What I bring: I'm 56 years old (almost 57), female, pursuing a Bachelor of Applied Science in Information Systems. I fall squarely into the demographic AARP (2025) identifies as skeptical of AI.

Why this matters: When my classmates see me using AI, they can't dismiss it as \"something for young tech bros.\" I'm their age, their peer. If I can learn AI collaboration at 56, the \"I'm too old\" excuse becomes harder to maintain. My age is an asset in bridge-building, not a barrier.

2. Technical Background

What I bring: 20+ years in IT infrastructure. I've managed mainframe operations, led 12-person teams at Dell Technologies and Jewish Hospital, supervised 24/7 datacenter operations across 3 states, and achieved 100% uptime during critical healthcare system migrations (McKesson/Cerner). I've worked with mainframes, Cisco networking, Linux, PHI compliance, and supported 5,000+ endpoints. Currently completing BAS-IS degree (graduation Spring 2026) with President's Scholar honors (9 terms, 3.94 GPA) and pursuing CAPM certification. Full background: aprilsykes.com

Why this matters: My technical fluency lowers my barriers to AI adoption. I'm comfortable experimenting with new tools, understanding system limitations, debugging errors. This gives me advantages that non-technical people lackbut it also means I might underestimate their barriers. I need to remember that \"just try ChatGPT\" feels different to someone without 20 years of troubleshooting experience.

3. Early Adopter Status

What I bring: I've been using AI since ChatGPT launched in November 20223 years of daily use. I've built prompting skills, developed trust through repeated interactions, and learned AI's strengths and limitations through trial and error.

Why this matters: I have knowledge and confidence that comes from 3 years of practice. But this creates a curse of knowledgewhat feels intuitive to me now was once unfamiliar. When explaining AI to classmates, I have to consciously remember what it felt like the first time I prompted ChatGPT. I can't assume they'll \"just get it.\"

4. Sociological Lens (Still Learning)

What I bring: This is my second sociology course (completed SOC101 in prior terms). I'm discovering frameworks through lived experience, not fitting experience into pre-existing academic boxes. I'm learning to see technology as social, not just technical.

Why this matters: My relative inexperience with sociology means I approach the AI literacy divide as a lived problem, not an abstract concept. I see my classmates' fear as real and worthy of empathy, not as irrational resistance to progress. My practitioner perspective complements sociological theory.

The Educator Role I've Taken On

After completing the H.C. Lombardo App, I shared my experience with classmates. Their reactionsfear, confusion, skepticismmade me realize I had accidentally positioned myself as an informal AI educator. This wasn't my intent, but it's the role I now occupy.

What This Role Looks Like

  • Addressing specific fears: \"Will AI replace me?\" \"It amplifies you. You're still the decision-maker.\"
  • Demystifying AI: \"It's not Skynet. It's pattern matching. Think autocomplete on steroids.\"
  • Staying present during experiments: \"Try asking it to explain this concept...\" (sitting with them)
  • Normalizing mistakes: \"Yeah, it gave me wrong database syntax last week. Here's how I caught it.\"
  • Sharing my limitations: \"I still verify everything. I lost 5 hours once trusting it blindly.\"
  • Translating jargon: Making technical concepts accessible to non-IT classmates

Why This Matters for the Divide

As discussed in the Strategies section, the three major approaches (Education, Policy, Technology) all assume people are ready to engage with AI. None adequately address the psychological barriersfear, distrust, lack of trusted guides.

Human bridges close the gap that programs can't. My classmates don't need just a free Google coursethey need someone they trust to walk them through their first interaction. Someone who:

  • Understands their fear because they were once there (I remember my first ChatGPT prompt)
  • Can answer questions in real-time without judgment
  • Shares both successes AND failures (the 5-hour loss, debugging struggles)
  • Represents their demographic (age 56, not 26)
  • Has credibility from lived experience (I built an app, not just took a course)

Biases I Need to Watch

1. Pro-AI Enthusiasm Bias

The bias: I LOVE using AI. Building the Lombardo app was exhilarating. I want everyone to experience that productivity boost.

How I'm addressing it: Remembering that my classmates' fear is rational given their lack of experience. Not dismissing concerns as \"resistance to change.\" Acknowledging AI's real limitations and risks. Sharing my failures alongside successes.

2. Technical Privilege Blind Spot

The bias: My 20 years in IT make troubleshooting feel natural. I might underestimate how intimidating technology is for non-technical people.

How I'm addressing it: Listening when classmates explain their barriers instead of assuming I know what they need. Remembering that \"just Google it\" assumes digital literacy I take for granted. Using plain language, not jargon.

3. Confirmation Bias (Finding What I Look For)

The bias: I'm researching the AI literacy divide while also trying to bridge it. Am I interpreting my classmates' reactions through the lens of my research framework?

How I'm addressing it: Documenting specific quotes and behaviors, not just my interpretations. Looking for evidence that contradicts my thesis (classmates who are neutral or curious, not just fearful). Being transparent about my positionality.

4. Savior Complex Risk

The bias: Framing myself as \"bridge-builder\" and \"educator\" could slide into \"I'm here to save the technologically illiterate.\" That's condescending.

How I'm addressing it: Recognizing that my classmates have valid reasons for AI avoidance. Not everyone needs to use AIsome choose not to for ethical, practical, or personal reasons. My role is to make AI accessible, not to evangelize or push adoption. Respecting autonomy.

The Observer Effect

The Timeline Complication

I started this research AFTER building the app and sharing it with classmates. Their fearful reactions prompted me to study the divide. This means my early interactions were \"natural\"I wasn't yet aware I was documenting research data. But now that I'm consciously studying the divide, am I behaving differently as an educator?

Questions I'm Wrestling With

  • Am I performing the educator role because I'm studying it? Or documenting a role I naturally fell into?
  • Does framing this as \"research\" change how my classmates interact with me?
  • Would I be as patient with their fears if I wasn't analyzing them sociologically?
  • Is my bridge-building authentic mentorship or strategic research positioning?

These tensions are unresolvable but need acknowledgment. The reflexivity itself is part of the data.

What I Can and Can't Claim

What I CAN Claim

  • I successfully learned AI collaboration at age 56 with 3 years of practice
  • My classmates (similar age/demographics) express fear and distrust of AI
  • The AI literacy divide is visible in my immediate community (SOC319 class)
  • Human intermediaries can address psychological barriers that programs/policies can't
  • My positionality (56, technical background, early adopter) gives me unique bridge-building potential

What I CAN'T Claim

  • My experience is representative of all 56-year-olds or all early adopters
  • Everyone can or should learn AI (some have valid reasons to abstain)
  • Technical background is necessary for AI literacy (it helps me, but isn't universal requirement)
  • My educator role is universally effective (I'm studying my specific context)
  • Bridging the divide is as simple as providing trusted guides (systemic barriers remain)

What I'm EXPLORING

  • Whether peer educators (same demographic as learners) are more effective than formal training
  • How sharing failures alongside successes affects trust-building in AI education
  • What makes someone a credible AI guide to skeptics (age? experience? technical background?)
  • Whether addressing psychological barriers requires lived experience crossing the divide
  • How informal mentorship complements formal AI literacy programs

Why My Position Matters for This Research

Autoethnography isn't about eliminating researcher biasit's about using my lived experience as data while being explicit about positionality. My specific background (56, technical, early adopter, sociology student) isn't a flaw in this research. It's the research context.

I'm not an objective observer studying \"the AI literacy divide\" from outside. I'm a bridge between the 34% and the 66%, with one foot in each world. This dual positioning gives me insight into both sides' perspectivesthe productivity gains AND the rational fears.

The insights are limited to my context but real. The generalizations are tentative but worth exploring. The reflexivity is honest (I hope). And the question remains: Can peer educators like me help close the literacy divide in ways that formal programs cannot?
