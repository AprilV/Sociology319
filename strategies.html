Strategies: Bridging the Divide

Three Existing Strategies to Address the AI Literacy Gap

The Challenge

With 66% of Americans never having used ChatGPT (Pew Research Center, 2025) and AI skills becoming essential, governments, organizations, and tech companies are trying different ways to help more people learn AI. This section looks at three major approaches being used:

  1. Education & Training Programs - Teaching AI literacy through formal and informal channels
  2. Policy & Regulation - Government mandates for transparency and accountability
  3. Technology Solutions - Making AI more accessible through better design

Each approach has strengths and limitations. None alone can solve the divide.

📚

Strategy 1: Education & Training Programs

What Is It?

Free and low-cost training programs are available to teach AI basics:

  • Google AI Essentials (Google, n.d.) - Free online course teaching how to write prompts and understand AI basics in under 5 hours
  • Microsoft AI Learning Path - Free beginner-level course covering AI fundamentals, generative AI, and practical applications (9.5 hours)
  • IBM AI Training - Free AI courses and certifications through IBM SkillsBuild
  • Coursera - Free AI courses from universities like Stanford, University of Michigan, and companies like DeepLearning.AI (some require paid certificates)
  • LinkedIn Learning - Over 15,000 AI courses including "What Is Generative AI?" with 2.3 million viewers (requires LinkedIn Premium or library card with access)
  • UC Berkeley Extension - Non-credit professional development courses including AI and machine learning topics
  • Public Libraries - Many offer free LinkedIn Learning access with a library card (check your local library's digital resources)

Who Benefits?

People who already want to learn. Mostly the 51-52% of college-educated Americans who have already tried ChatGPT (Pew Research Center, 2025).

Research shows these tend to be people who have:

  • Higher household incomes and formal education levels (Gelles-Watnick, 2024)
  • Home broadband access (95% of those earning $100,000+ vs. 57% earning under $30,000) (Gelles-Watnick, 2024)
  • Time and resources to dedicate to learning

My experience fits this: I had IT background, time for a 6-week project, knew AI was important, and had the equipment I needed. These programs would have helped me learn faster.

Who's Excluded?

Most Americans with high school education or less who haven't tried AI. Only 18% have used ChatGPT (Pew Research Center, 2025).

Research shows this group faces barriers like:

  • Lower broadband access - Only 57% of households earning under $30,000 have home broadband vs. 95% of those earning $100,000+ (Gelles-Watnick, 2024)
  • Rural internet gaps - 73% of rural adults have home broadband vs. 86% in suburban areas (Gelles-Watnick, 2024)
  • Age differences - Adults 65+ are less likely to own smartphones and use the internet (Gelles-Watnick, 2024)
  • Smartphone dependency - 28% of those earning under $30,000 rely only on smartphones for internet, making 10-20 hour courses difficult (Gelles-Watnick, 2024)

Also, 50% of Americans are more concerned than excited about AI (Pew Research Center, 2025), making them unlikely to seek out training.

What Works and What Doesn't

What works: Gives people actual skills. Free or cheap (Google, n.d.). Can reach lots of people online. Directly teaches what people need to know.

What doesn't work: Only helps people who already want to learn. Doesn't help with fear or distrust. Mostly reaches people who were already interested. Creates another gap between people who know about the training and people who don't.

What I noticed: Training programs like Google AI Essentials help, but they're not enough by themselves. My housemates need more than just a course. They need someone to help with their fear first. That's where my role as someone who can explain things (discussed in the Reflexivity section) becomes important.

Strategy 2: Policy & Regulation

What Is It?

Governments are implementing policies to make AI systems more transparent and accountable. Examples include:

  • EU AI Act (European Commission, 2024) - The world's first comprehensive AI regulation, requiring high-risk AI systems to be transparent, explainable, and subject to strict oversight including risk assessments, documentation, and human oversight measures
  • US Government Initiatives - Biden's Executive Order 14110 (Executive Office of the President, 2023) directing federal agencies to develop AI governance frameworks and standards, including the NIST AI Risk Management Framework (National Institute of Standards and Technology, 2023)
  • Algorithmic Accountability Measures - Proposed Algorithmic Accountability Act (Wyden, 2023) requiring organizations to document AI decision-making processes
  • Transparency Rights - Growing movement for consumers to understand AI-driven decisions affecting them

Who Benefits?

From what I can see, these policies mainly help people who already have advantages like people who:

  • Actually know these regulations exist and what their rights are
  • Have time and money to fight back if AI makes a bad decision about them
  • Work in areas like banking or hiring where there's more oversight
  • Live in places like the EU or US where governments are paying attention to this

The bigger picture: These policies force companies to explain how their AI works, which sounds good. But that only helps if regular people can actually understand those explanations and know how to use them.

Who's Excluded?

Based on what I've learned, a lot of people get left out:

  • People who don't even know about these laws - How can you use your rights if you don't know you have them?
  • Anyone using AI in unregulated areas - Like social media algorithms or online shopping recommendations that aren't covered by these rules
  • People in developing countries - These regulations are mostly US and EU focused
  • Workers at small companies - Regulators go after big corporations like Google or Amazon, not small businesses
  • People afraid to complain - If an AI system denies your loan or job application, fighting back takes time, money, and courage

Critical Analysis

Strengths: Creates accountability. Mandates transparency (European Commission, 2024; Executive Office of the President, 2023). Protects consumers from harmful AI. Incentivizes companies to explain their systems.

Limitations: Regulation alone doesn't create skills. While 95% of Americans have heard of AI, 53% can't detect AI-generated content (Pew Research Center, 2025). Transparency is useless if people lack literacy to understand explanations. Enforcement is inconsistent. Doesn't address adoption barriers. It only addresses accountability for existing systems.

My observation: Policy sets guardrails but doesn't teach people to drive. The EU AI Act (European Commission, 2024) and US Executive Order (Executive Office of the President, 2023) require transparency, but my classmates' fear won't be solved by regulation. They need hands-on experience and trusted guides.

Strategy 3: Technology Solutions

What Is It?

Tech companies are designing AI interfaces to be more accessible and user-friendly. Examples include:

  • Explainable AI (XAI) Tools (IBM, n.d.) - AI systems that reveal their reasoning process in plain language, helping users understand and trust AI-driven decisions through techniques like prediction accuracy tracing and decision transparency
  • Voice Interfaces - Conversational AI reducing typing barriers and improving accessibility
  • Low-Code/No-Code Platforms - Allowing non-programmers to build with AI (like me building the app!)
  • Simplified Prompting - ChatGPT's interface improvements reducing learning curve
  • Multi-language Support - AI systems expanding language availability for global access

Who Benefits?

From what I've seen, better design helps people who already have some comfort with technology like people who:

  • Know how to use smartphones or computers but don't know how to code
  • Prefer talking to typing (which also helps people with disabilities)
  • Get overwhelmed by complicated menus and need simple interfaces
  • Want to try new things but worry about "breaking" their computer

My experience with this: ChatGPT's conversational interface made AI accessible for me. I didn't need to learn Python first. I learned through using AI. When tools are easier to use, more people will try them.

Who's Excluded?

Based on what I've learned, making AI easier to use doesn't help everyone:

  • People who struggle with basic technology - If you can't use a smartphone or computer at all, a better interface won't help
  • People who are really scared of AI - No amount of friendly design fixes "Terminator" fears or worries about job loss
  • People who don't even know AI exists - A simpler interface doesn't matter if you never try it in the first place
  • People with certain disabilities - Many AI tools still don't work well with screen readers or other accessibility features
  • People without reliable internet - Most AI tools need internet access, so rural areas or low-income households get left out

Critical Analysis

Strengths: Reduces friction. Makes AI feel approachable. Explainable AI helps users understand decision-making (IBM, n.d.). Allows learning-by-doing. Benefits scale automatically as more people use improved interfaces.

Limitations: UX improvements ≠ solving root causes. ChatGPT's interface is already remarkably simple, yet only 34% have tried it and 50% remain more concerned than excited (Pew Research Center, 2025). Fear stops people before they see the interface. Even explainable AI (IBM, n.d.) doesn't address psychological barriers. 53% fear AI will erode creative thinking. No interface design fixes that.

My observation: ChatGPT's interface is already incredibly simple. My classmates' barrier isn't UX. It's fear and lack of understanding. Technology solutions alone can't bridge a cultural divide.

Comparing the Three Strategies

Strategy Strengths Limitations Primary Gap
Education Builds practical skills Requires motivation Doesn't address fear
Policy Creates accountability Doesn't teach skills Transparency understanding
Technology Reduces friction Assumes willingness Can't fix psychological barriers

The Critical Gap: All three strategies assume people are ready to engage with AI. None adequately address the fear, distrust, and cultural resistance that stop 66% of Americans from even trying. This is where human intermediarieseducators, mentors, trusted community membersbecome essential.

What's Missing: The Human Element

The people I've talked to don't need just training, regulation, or better UX. They need someone they trust to walk them through their first AI interaction. Someone who:

  • Addresses their specific fears ("Will it replace me?" "What if it's wrong?")
  • Demystifies AI ("It's not Skynet. It's pattern matching.")
  • Stays present during early experiments ("Try asking it this...")
  • Normalizes mistakes ("Yeah, it messed up. Here's how I caught it.")

This is the role I've taken on (explored in the Reflexivity section). Education, policy, and technology create infrastructure, but human bridges close the literacy divide.

⚖️ Is This Even a Problem? The Tech Optimist View

✅ The Counterargument: AI Will Democratize Access

Not everyone agrees there's a problem. Some technology leaders and AI companies argue that AI will reduce inequality, not increase it:

  • OpenAI's Sam Altman (2023) argues that advanced AI will democratize expertise. He envisions a future where anyone can access world-class tutoring, medical advice, and legal counsel through AI, regardless of income or geography. From this view, the "divide" is temporary and will close as AI becomes ubiquitous.
  • Microsoft's AI framework (Roach, 2023) emphasizes AI's potential to empower marginalized communities. They point to translation tools breaking language barriers, accessibility features helping people with disabilities, and AI assistants making professional tools accessible to non-experts. The solution, in their view, is more AI adoption, not less.

This perspective sees AI literacy as a temporary growing pain rather than a structural problem. Eventually, the thinking goes, AI will be so easy to use that literacy won't matter.

❌ Why I Disagree: The Divide is Getting Worse

The tech optimist view sounds great in theory. But the evidence shows the opposite happening:

  • 34% vs 66%: Two-thirds of Americans have never tried ChatGPT despite it being free and widely available for over two years (Pew, 2025). Access hasn't solved the problem.
  • Education gap widening: Those with postgraduate degrees are 2.9x more likely to use AI than those with high school education or less. The people who already have advantages are pulling further ahead.
  • Fear isn't going away: 50% of Americans are more concerned than excited about AI. Making AI "easier to use" doesn't address fear, distrust, or lack of a trusted guide.
  • Who benefits from "democratization"? When Microsoft and OpenAI talk about democratizing access, they mean selling subscriptions and expanding their market. The people being left behind 66% of Americans aren't their target audience.

My position: AI literacy is a structural inequality problem, not a temporary adoption curve. Without intentional intervention (education, mentorship, community support), the divide will deepen as AI becomes essential for work, education, and civic life. Technology alone doesn't fix social problems.
