Methodology: Studying the Divide from Within

Autoethnographic Approach to Understanding AI Literacy Gaps

Research Approach: Autoethnography

This research uses autoethnographysystematic analysis of personal experience to understand cultural or social phenomena. I'm studying my own journey crossing the AI literacy divide, combined with observations of my classmates who remain on the other side.

Why Autoethnography?

Advantages: Rich, detailed data about learning AI collaboration that would be hard to capture through surveys. Access to internal thought processesfear, trust development, breakthrough moments. Ability to document systematically over 6+ weeks. Lived experience from someone demographically in the \"skeptical\" group (age 56) but behaviorally in the \"literate\" group (34%).

Limitations: Single case studynot generalizable. Observer effects inevitable once I became aware this was research. My technical background gives me advantages not all 56-year-olds share. But these limitations are manageable through reflexivity and triangulation with classmate observations.

This isn't a controlled study. It's an exploratory investigation of one person's experience crossing the AI literacy divide, contextualized within broader patterns identified in existing research (Pew, AARP, MIT Sloan).

Data Sources

1. SOCIOLOGY.md Documentation (Primary Data)

What it is: 2,700+ lines documenting my 6-week collaboration with AI building the H.C. Lombardo App. Created organically as the project evolved, BEFORE I realized it was research data for this assignment.

What I analyzed:

  • Trust development: How I moved from verifying every AI suggestion to accepting patterns to collaborative shorthand
  • Learning trajectory: What AI taught me (Python, databases, APIs) vs. what I figured out through experimentation
  • Failure responses: How I handled the 5-hour work loss when AI made critical error
  • Language evolution: Shift from \"I'm building\" to \"we're building\" over 6 weeks
  • Breakthrough moments: When AI capabilities surprised me or changed my workflow

Available in project repository: github.com/AprilV/H.C.-Lombardo-App

2. GitHub Repository (Artifact Evidence)

What it is: 100+ Git commits showing development progression, problem-solving patterns, and collaboration evidence over 6 weeks.

What I analyzed:

  • Commit frequency and timing (intensity of collaboration)
  • Types of problems solved (complexity trajectory from simple queries to full-stack app)
  • Evidence of iteration (fixing AI errors, refining approaches)
  • Timestamps showing work patterns (late nights when stuck, rapid progress when AI helped)

3. Classmate Observations (Comparative Data)

What it is: Informal conversations with SOC319 classmates after sharing my H.C. Lombardo App experience. Not formal interviewsobservational data from natural interactions.

What I documented:

  • Specific fear statements: \"Isn't that cheating?\" \"AI is going to replace us all\" \"That's scary\"
  • Questions they asked (curiosity vs. skepticism)
  • Body language and emotional responses (visible discomfort, dismissiveness)
  • Who engaged further vs. who shut down the conversation
  • Demographic patterns (age, gender, tech background) where visible

Ethical note: No identifying information used. Quotes paraphrased. Focus on patterns, not individuals.

4. Academic Literature (Contextualizing Framework)

What it is: Five peer-reviewed and research organization sources (all 2025) providing statistics, patterns, and theoretical grounding for AI literacy divide.

  • Pew Research Center (2025): 34% adoption rate, demographic breakdowns, fear patterns
  • AARP (2025): Older adult skepticism and emerging adoption
  • MIT Sloan Management Review (2025): AI's impact on labor market

These sources provide the macro context (national statistics, demographic patterns) that my micro experience (one 56-year-old's journey) fits within.

Analysis Process

Step 1: Immersion in Literature (Week 1)

Read Pew, AARP, and MIT Sloan research on AI adoption gaps. Identified key patterns: 34% vs 66%, age-based skepticism, economic implications, psychological barriers. This gave me the conceptual framework for understanding my own experience.

Step 2: Retrospective Analysis of SOCIOLOGY.md (Weeks 2-3)

Went back through my 6 weeks of documentation looking for patterns:

  • Coded trust development phases (Testing Pattern Recognition Partnership)
  • Identified critical moments (first successful query, 5-hour failure, breakthrough understanding)
  • Tracked language evolution (\"I\" \"we\", technical collaborative framing)
  • Documented learning trajectory (what I knew Week 1 vs Week 6)

Step 3: Classmate Observation Documentation (Weeks 3-4)

After sharing my app experience, paid attention to classmates' reactions. Took notes immediately after conversations (not duringdidn't want to make anyone self-conscious). Looked for patterns in fears, questions, and engagement levels.

Step 4: Synthesis and Theming (Week 5)

Connected my micro experience to macro patterns:

  • My success (age 56, built app in 6 weeks) shows crossing the divide is possible for older adults
  • Classmates' fear mirrors AARP's findings about older adult skepticism
  • The gap between us demonstrates the 34% vs 66% divide in microcosm
  • Human bridges emerge as missing piece in Education/Policy/Technology strategies

Step 5: Reflexivity Check (Ongoing)

Throughout analysis, questioned my own biases: Am I overemphasizing success? Underestimating barriers for non-technical people? Seeing patterns because I'm looking for them? Used reflexivity section (previous page) to document these tensions explicitly.

Methodological Limitations

What This Study Cannot Claim

  • Generalizability: This is one person's journey. Others with different backgrounds, ages, or contexts would have different experiences.
  • Causality: I can't prove my technical background caused my success. Correlation causation.
  • Representativeness: My classmates aren't a random sample of the 66%. They're self-selected sociology students at a community college.
  • Objectivity: I'm studying myself and my community. Observer bias is inevitable despite reflexivity.
  • Completeness: 6 weeks is a snapshot. Long-term adoption patterns require longitudinal study.

Addressing Limitations Through Rigor

  • Triangulation: Multiple data sources (documentation, Git commits, observations, literature) strengthen findings
  • Reflexivity: Explicit acknowledgment of positionality and biases (see previous page)
  • Transparency: Making data available (SOCIOLOGY.md, GitHub repo) for others to evaluate
  • Modest claims: Framing this as exploratory, not definitive. Suggesting patterns, not proving laws.
  • Contextualization: Grounding personal experience in broader research (Pew, AARP, MIT Sloan)

What This Methodology Contributes

Existing research (Pew, AARP, MIT Sloan) provides quantitative data about the AI literacy divide: who uses AI, who doesn't, demographic patterns. This is essential macro-level understanding.

My autoethnographic approach provides qualitative depth: what it feels like to cross the divide at age 56, how trust develops through experimentation, why classmates resist despite seeing success, what role human bridges play in addressing psychological barriers.

This methodology bridges the gap between statistics (34% vs 66%) and lived experience (one person's 6-week journey from skeptic to literate). It's not generalizable, but it's rich, honest, and grounded in both personal data and scholarly research.
