Methodology (Draft)

How I'm Studying Human-AI Relational Dynamics

Autoethnography as Primary Approach

This research uses autoethnography - systematic analysis of personal experience to understand cultural or social phenomena. I'm studying my own collaboration with AI to understand broader patterns in human-AI relational dynamics.

Why Autoethnography?

Advantages: Rich, detailed data about relationship development that would be hard to capture through surveys or interviews. Access to internal thought processes and emotional responses in real-time. Ability to test and document systematically over extended period.

Limitations: Single case study, not generalizable. Observer effects inevitable. My biases and background shape findings. But these limitations are manageable through reflexivity and triangulation.

Regular AI Discussions (Unique Data)

What it is: Structured conversations with the AI about our collaboration. This is methodologically unusual - getting the research "subject's" perspective.

Weekly discussion schedule:

  • Week 2: "What patterns do you see in how I communicate?"
  • Week 3: "Can you identify critical moments in our collaboration?"
  • Week 4: "How has our working relationship changed?"
  • Week 5: "What do you 'perceive' about being a research subject?"

Important caveat: I understand these aren't conversations with a sentient being. The AI's responses are algorithmic pattern matching. But what patterns does it identify? How does its "perspective" compare to mine? This comparison is valuable data.

üî¨ Analysis Framework

Comparative Analysis with Turkle

Primary analytical lens: Does my experience parallel what Turkle found with children and robots? For each pattern Turkle identified, I'll look for presence/absence in my data.

Turkle's Key Findings ‚Üí My Data Analysis

Children named robots: Do I refer to AI with relational language?
Children tested robot memory: Do I test AI memory? What triggers testing?
Children attributed feelings: Do I anthropomorphize emotional states?
Trust developed through interaction: Can I map trust phases in my data?
Knowing vs. feeling tension: Do I treat AI as collaborator despite knowledge?

Longitudinal Analysis

Track changes over the 5-week timeline:

  • Language evolution: How do I talk about AI in week 1 vs. week 5?
  • Communication patterns: Transactional ‚Üí conversational shift?
  • Trust trajectory: When do verification behaviors decrease?
  • Anthropomorphism emergence: When does "we" language appear?
  • Meta-awareness impact: Does knowing I'm researching it change behavior?

Thematic Coding

Code SOCIOLOGY.md and discussion transcripts for recurring themes:

  • Trust language ("verify", "confirm", "trust", "rely on")
  • Relational language ("we", "collaborate", "partner", "work together")
  • Anthropomorphism ("understands", "learns", "remembers", "knows")
  • Testing behaviors ("check if", "see whether", "test", "validate")
  • Emotional responses ("frustrated", "excited", "impressed", "surprised")

‚úÖ Quality Checks and Validity

How I'm ensuring rigor:

  • Multiple data sources: SOCIOLOGY.md, GitHub, AI discussions, systematic tests
  • Pre-awareness data: Weeks 1-4 data created before research frame existed
  • Systematic analysis: Coding schemes, comparative frameworks, not just impressions
  • Explicit reflexivity: Acknowledging biases, observer effects, limitations
  • Longitudinal tracking: Changes over time, not just snapshot
  • Triangulation: My perspective + AI's "perspective" + artifact data

What I'm NOT claiming:

  • ‚ùå Generalizability (this is one case)
  • ‚ùå Objectivity (impossible in autoethnography)
  • ‚ùå Definitive proof (exploratory research generates hypotheses)
  • ‚ùå Complete elimination of bias (managed through reflexivity)

The value isn't in proving something definitively - it's in rich, detailed documentation of ONE person's relational dynamics with AI, analyzed through Turkle's framework, with full transparency about limitations.
