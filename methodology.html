How I'm Doing This Research

Methods & Sources (In Progress)

The Method: Autoethnography

Basically: I'm studying my own experience working with AI and trying to find patterns that might apply to other people too.

What autoethnography means: You analyze your own experience to understand bigger social/cultural stuff. Instead of pretending to be objective, you acknowledge that YOUR perspective gives you insight‚Äîespecially when studying something new that not many people have experienced yet.

"I realized I was living the phenomenon I was studying. My collaboration with AI wasn't just background to my sociology research‚Äîit WAS the sociology research."

Why This Method Makes Sense (I Think)

AI is Super New

Human-AI collaboration is happening RIGHT NOW. Traditional research takes years. By studying my own experience in real-time, I can document patterns as they emerge.

Internal Experience Matters

A lot of AI collaboration is psychological‚Äîtrust, cognitive dissonance, emotional responses. You can't really observe that from outside. Studying my own thoughts gives access to that.

I Have Detailed Data

Over 4 weeks I documented everything (553+ lines in SOCIOLOGY.md, 50+ git commits, conversation transcripts). That's way more detail than I'd get from interviewing someone else.

I Understand Both Sides

I'm technical enough to understand the AI, but sociology-minded enough to analyze the social patterns. That dual perspective is kind of unique.

Concern: Is studying just ONE person's experience (mine) enough? Need to address limitations about generalizability. Maybe mention that this is exploratory research that could guide future studies with larger samples?

How I'm Analyzing It

Using sociological theories from the course:

Social Exchange Theory

The idea: People calculate costs vs. benefits in relationships.

How I'm using it: Analyzing why I kept using AI (benefits like productivity outweighed costs like uncertainty).

Symbolic Interactionism

The idea: Meaning gets created through interaction and communication.

How I'm using it: Looking at how we developed shared language and understanding over time.

Turkle's Work

The idea: Technology makes us more isolated from each other.

How I'm using it: My experience seems different‚ÄîI'm documenting collaboration, not isolation. Interesting contrast.

Digital Divide Theory

The idea: Inequality in tech access and skills.

How I'm using it: Extending it to AI literacy‚Äînot just "do you have AI" but "can you use it effectively?"

Limitations (Being Honest)

The assignment asks us to acknowledge what our research CAN'T do:

Just One Person

Problem: Studying only my experience might not apply to everyone else.

My response: This is exploratory research‚Äîfinding patterns that future studies with more people can test. It's a starting point.

Could Be Biased

Problem: I might remember things wrong or interpret stuff to fit what I want to prove.

My response: That's why I documented in real-time and have objective records (commits, transcripts) to check against.

I'm Privileged

Problem: I have advantages (university, tech background, time, support) that most people don't. My success might not be replicable.

My response: That's literally part of what I'm studying‚Äîinequality in who can collaborate effectively. The privilege IS the point.

Short Timeframe

Problem: Only 4 weeks‚Äîcollaboration patterns might change over longer time.

My response: Acknowledging this is early-stage collaboration. Long-term research would be interesting follow-up.

‚Üí These limitations don't make the research useless‚Äîthey just mean I need to be clear about what I CAN and CAN'T claim

Ethical Considerations

üîí

Privacy and Consent

Third-party reactions (classmates, family) are reported without identifying information. Quotes are paraphrased to protect privacy while preserving meaning. Public GitHub repositories contain only technical content, not personal information.

üéØ

Representation and Voice

Autoethnography risks privileging researcher's perspective. This study acknowledges that my experience represents one position (early adopter, technically skilled, educationally privileged) and does not claim to represent all AI users or non-users.

üí≠

AI Agency and Anthropomorphization

Discussion of "collaboration" and "partnership" with AI requires ethical care. This research uses these terms descriptively (patterns resemble human collaboration) without claiming AI possesses consciousness, agency, or rights. Maintaining this distinction prevents both over-attribution and dismissal of significant sociotechnical dynamics.

‚öñÔ∏è

Inequality and Responsibility

Researching my own successful collaboration while others struggle raises ethical questions about privilege and responsibility. This research responds by making knowledge openly accessible, advocating for systemic change, and explicitly analyzing structural inequalities rather than attributing outcomes to individual merit.

Ethical Stuff I'm Thinking About

The assignment asks us to consider ethics:

When I mention reactions from classmates or family, I'm not using names or identifying info. Just paraphrasing what they said while keeping the meaning.

Who I Represent

I'm clear that this is MY experience (early adopter, technically skilled, privileged). Not claiming to represent everyone. Just one person's perspective.

AI Language

When I say "collaboration" and "partnership" with AI‚ÄîI mean patterns RESEMBLE human collaboration. Not saying AI is conscious or has feelings. Descriptive, not literal.

Inequality & Responsibility

I'm documenting advantages I have. Not saying "everyone can do this if they try hard enough"‚Äîthat ignores structural barriers. Acknowledging privilege matters.
