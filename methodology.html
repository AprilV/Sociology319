Methodology: Studying the Divide from Within

Autoethnographic Approach to Understanding AI Literacy Gaps

🔍 The Research Question

How does someone cross the AI literacy divide? And what can that journey teach us about the barriers keeping 66% of Americans from even trying?

This research uses my multi-year experience using AI as a collaborative partner in building multiple applications across different programming languages. This is combined with observations of classmates who remain on the other side of the divide. This autoethnographic approach captures the lived experience of crossing the digital divide that statistics alone cannot reveal.

Research Approach: Mixed-Methods Design

This research combines three complementary methodologies to understand the AI literacy divide from multiple angles:

1. Autoethnography

Qualitative: Systematic analysis of my multi-year journey using AI to build applications across different programming languages and frameworks

2. Secondary Data

Quantitative: Analysis of Pew Research statistics on national AI adoption patterns

3. Literature Review

Theoretical: Applying digital divide theory (Robinson, Dwivedi) to interpret findings

Why Mixed Methods?

Autoethnography alone would give rich detail but lack generalizability. Statistics alone would show patterns but miss lived experience. Theory alone would provide framework but no current data.

Together, they create a complete picture: my micro experience (one 56-year-old's multi-year AI collaboration journey) contextualized within macro patterns (66% national non-adoption) and interpreted through established theory (digital inequalities 2.0).

This isn't a controlled study. It's an exploratory mixed-methods investigation combining personal narrative, national statistics, and sociological theory to understand what it means to cross the AI literacy divide.

Data Sources

1. SOCIOLOGY.md Documentation (Primary Data)

What it is: 2,700+ lines documenting my 6-week collaboration with AI building the H.C. Lombardo App. Created organically as the project evolved, BEFORE I realized it was research data for this assignment.

What I analyzed:

  • Trust development: How I moved from verifying every AI suggestion to accepting patterns to collaborative shorthand
  • Learning trajectory: What AI taught me (Python, databases, APIs) vs. what I figured out through experimentation
  • Failure responses: How I handled the 5-hour work loss when AI made critical error
  • Language evolution: Shift from "I'm building" to "we're building" over 6 weeks
  • Breakthrough moments: When AI capabilities surprised me or changed my workflow

2. GitHub Repository (Artifact Evidence)

What it is: 100+ Git commits showing development progression, problem-solving patterns, and collaboration evidence over 6 weeks.

What I analyzed:

  • Commit frequency and timing (intensity of collaboration)
  • Types of problems solved (complexity trajectory from simple queries to full-stack app)
  • Evidence of iteration (fixing AI errors, refining approaches)
  • Timestamps showing work patterns (late nights when stuck, rapid progress when AI helped)

3. Informal Observations (Comparative Data)

What it is: Informal conversations with classmates and others over time about AI use. These are fellow students pursuing bachelor's degrees who I've known for over a year through various courses. Discussions occurred naturally in different contexts, including classroom discussions with professors.

What I observed:

  • Common fear statements: concerns about cheating, job replacement, and general apprehension about AI
  • Patterns in questions (curiosity vs. skepticism)
  • Emotional responses (visible discomfort, dismissiveness, engagement)
  • Who engaged further vs. who avoided the topic
  • Demographic patterns (age, gender, tech background) where apparent

Ethical note: No identifying information used. Quotes paraphrased. Focus on patterns, not individuals.

3. Academic Literature (Contextualizing Framework)

What it is: Five peer-reviewed and research organization sources (all 2024-2025) providing statistics, patterns, and theoretical grounding for AI literacy divide.

  • Pew Research Center (2025): 34% adoption rate, demographic breakdowns, fear patterns, older adult skepticism
  • World Economic Forum (2024): Future of jobs and AI's impact on labor market
  • McKinsey (2024): State of AI organizational adoption and value creation

These sources provide the macro context (national statistics, demographic patterns) that my micro experience (one 56-year-old's multi-year journey) fits within.

Analysis Process

Step 1: Immersion in Literature

Read Pew Research Center, World Economic Forum, and McKinsey research on AI adoption gaps. Identified key patterns: 34% vs 66%, age-based skepticism, economic implications, psychological barriers. This gave me the conceptual framework for understanding my own multi-year experience.

Step 2: Reflective Analysis of Personal Experience

Reflected on my years of AI collaboration experience looking for patterns:

  • How trust developed over sustained use across multiple projects
  • What I learned about creating effective AI collaboration environments
  • How my technical capabilities evolved with AI partnership
  • Critical moments when AI changed my approach to problem-solving
  • Patterns in what works vs. what doesn't when working with AI

Step 3: Observation and Informal Conversations

Over time, observed classmates' reactions when discussing AI use and my technical work. Had informal conversations with classmates, housemates, and others about their views on AI. Mentally noted patterns in reactions, fears, questions, and engagement levels. These observations occurred naturally across multiple courses and contexts throughout my bachelor's degree program.

Step 4: Synthesis and Theming

Connected my micro experience to macro patterns:

  • My sustained AI use (age 56, building multiple applications over years) shows crossing the divide is possible for older adults with technical background
  • Classmates' fear mirrors Pew Research Center's findings about older adult skepticism
  • The gap between us demonstrates the 34% vs 66% divide in microcosm
  • Human bridges emerge as missing piece in Education/Policy/Technology strategies

Step 5: Reflexivity Check (Ongoing)

Throughout analysis, questioned my own biases: Am I overemphasizing success? Underestimating barriers for non-technical people? Seeing patterns because I'm looking for them? Used reflexivity section (previous page) to document these tensions explicitly.

Methodological Limitations

What This Study Cannot Claim

  • Generalizability: This is one person's journey. Others with different backgrounds, ages, or contexts would have different experiences.
  • Causality: I can't prove my technical background caused my success. Correlation ≠ causation.
  • Representativeness: My classmates aren't a random sample of the 66%. They're IT students at a community college.
  • Objectivity: I'm studying myself and my community. Observer bias is inevitable despite reflexivity.
  • Memory limitations: Reflecting on years of experience relies on memory, which can be selective or reconstructed.

How I Tried to Be Fair and Honest

  • Multiple sources: Used my own experience, observations, and research data together
  • Being upfront: Acknowledged my biases and perspective
  • Context: Described my background so readers understand where I'm coming from
  • Realistic scope: This is one student's observations over a term, not a scientific study
  • Backing it up: Connected what I noticed to actual research (Pew, McKinsey, etc.)

What This Adds

Research like Pew and McKinsey tell us who uses AI and who doesn't, with statistics and demographics.

My project adds the personal side: what it's actually like to use AI at 56, how I learned to work with it over time, and why my classmates seem hesitant even though they see it working for me.

It's just one person's experience, but it puts a face on the statistics and connects what researchers found to what I've lived.
