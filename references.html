References

Academic Sources for This Research

Primary Theoretical Framework

Turkle, Sherry. (2011). Alone Together: Why We Expect More from Technology and Less from Each Other. Basic Books.

Focus: Part Two ‚Äî "Of Robots and Moonlight" (pp. 19-154). Turkle's ethnographic research on children developing relational bonds with robots (AIBO, Furby, Paro, My Real Baby). Introduces concepts of "as-if relationships," "evocative objects," "the robotic moment," and "authenticity vs. performance."

Supporting Sources

1. Social Responses to Computers

Nass, Clifford, and Youngme Moon. (2000). "Machines and Mindlessness: Social Responses to Computers." Journal of Social Issues, 56(1), 81-103.

Why this source: Foundational CASA (Computers As Social Actors) research demonstrating humans unconsciously apply social norms to computers. Explains why I treat AI with politeness, reciprocity, and partnership framing despite knowing it's algorithmic.

Key concepts: Media equation, mindless application of social rules, computer as social actor

2. Autoethnography Method

Ellis, Carolyn, Tony E. Adams, and Arthur P. Bochner. (2011). "Autoethnography: An Overview." Forum: Qualitative Social Research, 12(1), Article 10.

Why this source: Legitimizes autoethnographic methodology‚Äîstudying one's own experience as valid sociological research. Addresses reflexivity, positionality, and the researcher-as-subject approach I'm using.

Access: Free online at qualitative-research.net

3. Anthropomorphism & Trust

Waytz, Adam, Joy Heafner, and Nicholas Epley. (2014). "The Mind in the Machine: Anthropomorphism Increases Trust in an Autonomous Vehicle." Journal of Experimental Social Psychology, 52, 113-117.

Why this source: Empirical evidence that anthropomorphizing technology increases trust and reliance. Directly supports my observed trust development pattern (Week 1: verify everything ‚Üí Week 6: implement without checking).

Key finding: Attributing mental states to machines increases user trust, even when users know the machine isn't conscious

4. Long-Term Human-Computer Relationships

Bickmore, Timothy W., and Rosalind W. Picard. (2005). "Establishing and Maintaining Long-Term Human-Computer Relationships." ACM Transactions on Computer-Human Interaction, 12(2), 293-327.

Why this source: Research on how humans develop ongoing relationships with AI agents over time. Distinguishes between transactional interactions vs. relational dynamics‚Äîmatching my communication evolution from "create function X" to "we need to handle standings."

Key concepts: Relational agents, social-emotional communication, relationship maintenance strategies

5. GitHub Copilot Collaboration Study

Barke, Shraddha, Michael B. James, and Nadia Polikarpova. (2023). "Grounded Copilot: How Programmers Interact with Code-Generating Models." Proceedings of the ACM on Programming Languages, 7(OOPSLA1), 85-111.

Why this source: Recent empirical research on GitHub Copilot specifically‚Äîthe exact tool I'm using. Documents how programmers collaborate with AI pair programming assistants, trust calibration, and verification behaviors.

Relevance: Provides workplace context for adult-AI collaboration, distinguishing it from Turkle's children-robot play scenarios

6. [Alternative if Copilot study unavailable]

Jarrahi, Mohammad Hossein, et al. (2019). "Artificial Intelligence and the Future of Work: Human-AI Symbiosis in Organizational Decision Making." Business Horizons, 62(4), 577-586.

Backup option: Broader workplace AI collaboration research if the Copilot-specific study is hard to access. Discusses human-AI symbiosis, complementary strengths, and collaboration dynamics in professional contexts.
