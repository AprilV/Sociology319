The Problem (Draft)

The AI Literacy Divide

What the Problem Actually Is

Everyone I know has access to AI tools. ChatGPT is free. GitHub Copilot is free for students. There are dozens of AI tools available to anyone with an internet connection. But when I talk to people about using AI‚Äîclassmates, family, random people I meet‚Äîthey respond in really different ways. Most people are either scared, confused about whether it's "cheating," or have tried it once and given up because they didn't know what to do with it.

"Most people I've talked to about AI are either: 1) Scared of it ('Terminator' references, job loss fears), 2) Dismissive of it ('it's just a fancy autocomplete'), or 3) Have never really engaged with it beyond ChatGPT for homework (which they feel guilty about)."
‚Äî From my notes, Week 3

So the problem isn't about access to technology‚Äîit's about knowing how to use it effectively and being psychologically ready to try. This looks like a new kind of inequality. Not the traditional "digital divide" we learned about (some people have internet, some don't), but something different‚Äîa skills and mindset gap.

Why This Is Different from the Regular Digital Divide

  • Everyone has access. AI tools are free or cheap. You don't need expensive equipment. So it's not about money or having technology.
  • It's about skills and mindset. The barrier is knowing what to do and not being afraid to try. Like owning a guitar versus knowing how to play it.
  • Psychological factors matter. Fear, ethical concerns, not understanding what AI even is‚Äîthese stop people more than lack of access.
