Context: The AI Literacy Divide

Literature Review and Problem Definition

What is the AI Literacy Divide?

The AI literacy divide refers to the growing gap between those who can effectively use artificial intelligence tools and those who cannot or will not adopt them. Unlike previous digital divides focused on access to hardware or internet connectivity, this divide centers on skills, knowledge, and psychological readiness to collaborate with AI systems.

As AI becomes integrated into education, employment, and daily life, this divide threatens to create a new class of technological \"haves\" and \"have-nots\"not based on who owns a computer, but on who can leverage AI to amplify their capabilities.

The Numbers Tell the Story

34% of U.S. adults have used ChatGPT, according to Pew Research Center's June 2025 survey.

(Pew Research Center, 2025)

This means 66% of Americanstwo-thirds of the populationhave never used the most accessible AI tool available. The divide is even starker when we examine who is in that 34%: younger, college-educated, higher-income individuals are significantly overrepresented (Pew Research Center, 2025).

Age and Skepticism

AARP's 2025 research reveals that while older adults embrace many technologies, they remain deeply skeptical of AI. Despite this skepticism, some older adults are using AI tools, driven by necessity or curiosity (AARP, 2025). This creates an internal divide within demographics: early adopters who experiment with AI versus those who avoid it entirely due to fear, distrust, or lack of knowledge.

The result? As AI skills become essential for employment, civic participation, and even basic tasks, those who cannot or will not learn risk being left behind economically and socially.

Why This Divide Matters

1. Economic Inequality

AI is rapidly transforming the U.S. labor market, with workers who can use AI tools seeing productivity gains and career advantages (MIT Sloan Management Review, 2025). Those without AI skills face potential job displacement or wage stagnation as employers increasingly expect AI competency across rolesfrom administrative work to creative fields.

2. Educational Disparities

Students who learn to collaborate with AI can complete assignments faster, learn more efficiently, and develop problem-solving skills that their peers lack. Without equitable access to AI literacy education, existing educational inequalities will deepen, creating a two-tiered system where some students have AI assistants while others struggle alone.

3. Psychological Barriers

Fear plays a major role in the divide. Many non-users fear AI will replace them, make mistakes they'll be blamed for, or represent threats to humanity (\"Terminator\" scenarios). Both the general public and AI experts express concerns about AI's impact, though their specific fears differ (Pew Research Center, 2025). Addressing the literacy gap requires confronting these psychological barriers, not just providing technical training.

4. Civic Participation

As AI systems make decisions about credit, hiring, criminal justice, and more, citizens need AI literacy to understand how these systems work, identify bias, and advocate for fairness. Without widespread AI literacy, algorithmic governance risks becoming a black box that only elites can understand or challenge.

The Divide in Practice

AI Literate (34%)

  • Use ChatGPT or similar tools regularly
  • Understand how to prompt AI effectively
  • See AI as collaborator, not threat
  • Experiment and iterate with AI
  • Leverage AI to amplify productivity
  • Understand AI's limitations

Non-Users (66%)

  • Have never tried ChatGPT or AI tools
  • Fear AI will replace them or make errors
  • Lack knowledge of how AI works
  • No trusted guide to learn from
  • Fall further behind as AI advances
  • May face job displacement

This is not a temporary gap. As AI tools become more sophisticated, the advantage held by the 34% grows exponentially, while the 66% face increasing barriers to entry.

The Central Question

As AI literacy becomes essential to economic opportunity, education, and civic participation, how do we ensure equitable access across age, income, and education levels?
