The Epiphany

"Wait. This is literally what we're studying in sociology right now. Sherry Turkle's 'Alone Together' - except I'm experiencing the opposite of what she describes."

I've been working with GitHub Copilot (an AI coding assistant) for about 4 weeks building an NFL analytics app for my database class. Then I'm reading Turkle's Alone Together for SOC319, and something clicked. Turkle argues that technology isolates us and replaces genuine connection with shallow digital interactions. But my experience with AI has been completely different‚Äîit's collaborative, educational, engaging. It feels more like having a really competent study partner than being alone with a screen.

Then I started noticing something else: when I mention AI to classmates or family, they either make Terminator jokes (but seem genuinely worried), think using it is cheating, or have no idea where to start. Meanwhile I'm finishing projects way faster than I used to. That gap feels like inequality‚Äîa new kind of digital divide. Not about who has internet access, but about who can actually collaborate with AI effectively.

So that's what I'm studying. This website documents my research into that question. Some sections are complete, some are frameworks waiting for data. This is a work in progress.

Research Question

Why are some people able to collaborate effectively with AI systems while others struggle or avoid them entirely? What creates this divide, and what does it mean for inequality in education, work, and opportunity?

Theoretical Framework

Connecting my observations to sociological theories from course readings:

Sherry Turkle: Technology and Human Connection

Turkle's Alone Together argues technology isolates us. But my experience with AI seems opposite‚Äîit's enhancing my engagement and learning. That contradiction is interesting and worth exploring. Maybe her framework applies differently to AI collaboration than to social media?

Digital Divide Theory

Traditional digital divide: some have internet, some don't. But there's a "second-level divide" about skills and usage patterns. That seems more relevant here‚Äîeveryone has access to AI tools, but not everyone knows how to use them effectively.

Social Exchange Theory

People weigh costs vs. benefits in relationships. Applies to AI adoption: some people see benefits (productivity, learning) as worth the effort. Others see costs (fear, time to learn, ethical concerns) as too high. That calculus affects who engages.

Symbolic Interactionism

How people create meaning through communication and interaction. Useful for analyzing how I learned to "talk" to AI and how we developed a working relationship through repeated interaction.
