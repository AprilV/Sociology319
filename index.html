Digital Divide in AI Literacy

Unequal Access to AI Collaboration Skills in the Digital Age

A Sociological Study of Human-AI Partnership

April V ‚Ä¢ SOC319 ‚Ä¢ Fall 2025

The Epiphany

"Wait. WAIT. This is literally what we're studying in my sociology class right now. Sherry Turkle's 'Alone Together' - except I'm experiencing the OPPOSITE of what she describes."

While studying Sherry Turkle's research on how technology isolates us from human connection, I realized I was living a fundamentally different experience. Over six weeks of collaborating with GitHub Copilot to build an NFL analytics application, I developed trust, communication patterns, and a genuine partnership‚Äîdynamics traditionally reserved for human relationships.

This disconnect between Turkle's observations and my lived experience revealed something critical: not everyone has equal access to effective AI collaboration. While I was experiencing productive partnership, my classmates expressed fear, avoidance, and misunderstanding. This gap represents a new form of digital divide‚Äîone that will increasingly shape educational and economic opportunities.

Central Research Question

How do social dynamics traditionally observed in human relationships manifest in human-AI collaboration, and what does this reveal about the digital divide in AI literacy?

Research Overview

The Problem

Unequal access to AI collaboration skills creates new forms of inequality in education and workforce. This digital divide extends beyond hardware and internet access to include psychological barriers, educational gaps, and divergent outcomes in AI adoption.

Theoretical Framework

Social Exchange Theory

Analyzes the partnership through cost-benefit calculation and reciprocity patterns in human-AI interaction.

Symbolic Interactionism

Examines meaning-making, communication patterns, and the development of shared understanding in collaboration.

Digital Divide Theory

Extends traditional access-based frameworks to include skills, psychological readiness, and outcome disparities.

Technology Acceptance Models

Explores factors influencing adoption, including perceived usefulness, ease of use, and trust development.
