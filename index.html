Digital Divide in AI Literacy

Unequal Access to AI Collaboration Skills in the Digital Age

A Sociological Study of Human-AI Partnership

April V ‚Ä¢ SOC319 ‚Ä¢ Fall 2025

Research Context

"Wait. This is literally what we're studying in sociology right now. Sherry Turkle's 'Alone Together' - except I'm experiencing the opposite of what she describes."

For the past four weeks, I have been working with GitHub Copilot, an AI coding assistant, to build an NFL analytics application for another course. While reading Turkle's Alone Together for SOC319, I noticed a disconnect. Turkle describes isolation, emotional dependency, and degraded human connection through technology. My experience with AI collaboration does not match that pattern. The relationship functions more like a professional working partnership than the problematic dynamics Turkle documents.

This observation led to a second realization. When I discuss AI use with classmates or family, their responses cluster into three categories: fear (often invoking "Terminator" scenarios), ethical concern (viewing AI assistance as academic dishonesty), or complete unfamiliarity with how to use these tools effectively. This disparity may represent a new form of digital divide.

Note: This website documents an ongoing research project. Sections marked "in progress" or "draft" indicate areas where source collection and analysis are still underway.

Research Question

How do social dynamics observable in human relationships manifest in human-AI collaboration, and what do these patterns reveal about differential access to effective AI literacy?

Trust Development Patterns

Collaboration evolved through observable stages. Initial interactions involved extensive verification of AI output. After four weeks, verification decreased as reliability was established. This mirrors trust formation patterns in human professional relationships.

Communication Evolution

Early instructions were explicit and procedural. Over time, communication became more contextual and less detailed. This shift suggests either improved user skill in prompt construction or system adaptation to user patterns‚Äîlikely both.

Theories I'm Planning to Use (and Why)

The assignment asks us to use sociological theories to explain what we're observing. Here's what I think might apply to AI collaboration:

Social Exchange Theory

Why: This is about weighing costs vs. benefits in relationships. I can use it to explain why some people try AI (benefits seem worth it) and why others avoid it (costs like fear or time feel too high).

Symbolic Interactionism

Why: This theory looks at how people communicate and create shared meanings. Seems relevant because working with AI is all about figuring out how to communicate effectively.

Digital Divide Theory

Why: Obviously relevant since my whole project is about AI creating inequality. Traditional digital divide is about internet access‚Äîmine is about skills and psychological barriers.
