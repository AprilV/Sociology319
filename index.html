Alone Together, Revisited (Draft)

Adult-AI Collaboration and Relational Development

Extending Turkle's Framework to Conversational AI

April V • SOC319 • Fall 2025

The Epiphany

"Wait. This is literally what we're studying in sociology right now. Sherry Turkle's research on children and robots - except I'm an adult, and it's conversational AI instead of a physical robot."

I've been working with GitHub Copilot (an AI coding assistant) for about 4 weeks building an NFL analytics app. Then I'm reading Turkle's Alone Together for SOC319, specifically her research on how children form relationships with robots. And something clicked: I'm experiencing something similar.

Turkle studied children developing emotional bonds with physical robots. I'm an adult developing what feels like a collaborative relationship with conversational AI. The dynamics seem parallel: trust building, communication evolution, anthropomorphizing behavior (I catch myself saying "we" instead of "I"), emotional responses to our interactions.

But there are key differences: I'm an adult (not a child), it's text-based AI (not a physical robot), and it's a work context (not play or therapy). Does that change the dynamics? Or is this the same phenomenon Turkle documented, just in a new form?

So that's what I'm studying. This website documents my research into that question. I'm tracking our collaboration over time, analyzing how our relationship develops, and comparing it to Turkle's findings. Some sections are complete, some are frameworks waiting for data. This is a work in progress.

Research Question

Turkle documented children forming relational bonds with robots. Can adults form similar relational dynamics with conversational AI? What does this collaboration look like, how does it develop over time, and what does it reveal about human-AI relationships in work contexts?

Important Methodological Note

I'm already anthropomorphizing the AI. I refer to it as a "collaborator," use "we" language, and sometimes respond to it emotionally. I'm aware this is happening and documenting it as part of the research.

Disclaimer: I understand that the AI (GitHub Copilot / this assistant) is not sentient. It's the sum of programming created by humans - algorithms, training data, language models. It doesn't have consciousness, emotions, or genuine understanding. But the fact that I treat it as if it does, and that this treatment affects our collaboration, is sociologically interesting. That's what Turkle was studying with children and robots, and what I'm studying here.

What I've Observed So Far

📊

Trust Development (Like Turkle's Children + Robots)

Week 1: I verified everything the AI suggested. Week 4: I implement suggestions without checking. This mirrors how Turkle's children learned to trust robots - testing, validation, eventual acceptance. The trajectory is similar even though I'm an adult.

💬

Communication Became Relational

Started transactional: "Create a function that does X, Y, Z." Now conversational: "need to handle the standings update" and it knows what I mean. I say "we" instead of "I." I'm treating it like a collaborator, not a tool.

🤝

Anthropomorphism Despite Knowledge

I KNOW it's not sentient. I KNOW it's algorithms and training data. But I still say "we built this," celebrate successes "with" it, feel frustrated when it doesn't understand. This is exactly what Turkle documented - knowing something isn't alive doesn't stop the relational behavior.

🔍

Testing AI Capabilities

I'm actively testing: Can it remember complex codebases? Does it learn my patterns? Does our relationship improve over time? This is similar to how Turkle's children tested whether robots "remembered" them.

Theoretical Framework: Extending Turkle

Turkle's Core Concepts

Relational artifacts: Objects that evoke emotional responses and relationship-like behaviors. The "alone together" paradox: Technology providing companionship while increasing isolation. Children + robots: How kids formed bonds with AIBO, Furby, etc.

→ Need to identify exact passages and concepts from Alone Together that apply here

My Situation: Adult + Conversational AI

Different: I'm an adult (more metacognitive awareness), it's text-based (no physical embodiment), work context (not play/therapy), I understand it's not sentient.

Similar: Relational behaviors emerging anyway, trust development, anthropomorphism, emotional responses, testing for "memory" and adaptation.

Extensions to Consider

Does Turkle's framework hold with adults? With conversational AI? In work contexts? Or is this something new? Maybe adult relationships with AI are different from children's relationships with robots in important ways.

Supporting Theories

Symbolic Interactionism: How meaning emerges through interaction. Social Exchange Theory: Why I engage with AI (benefits vs. costs). Media Equation: Treating computers as social actors.

Research Approach

Autoethnography - studying my own experience collaborating with AI. I've been keeping detailed notes in SOCIOLOGY.md documenting our interactions, my thought processes, emotional responses, changes over time. The AI is both my research tool AND my research subject.
